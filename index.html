How Conversion Formulas Work

Most unit conversions use a simple multiplication by a conversion factor. For example, to convert kilometers to miles, you multiply by 0.621371:

Formula:
miles = km × 0.621371
e.g., 10 km × 0.621371 = 6.21371 miles

Some conversions (like temperature) require additional steps beyond multiplication, using offset formulas:

Formula:
°F = (°C × 9/5) + 32
e.g., 100°C × 1.8 + 32 = 212°F

SI System Guide

The 7 SI Base Units Explained

The International System of Units (SI), from the French Système International d'Unités, is the modern standardized form of the metric system. Established by the General Conference on Weights and Measures (CGPM), SI is the world's most widely used measurement system, underpinning science, industry, and commerce globally.

The 7 Base Units

SI defines 7 fundamental base units from which all other units are derived:

1. Metre (m) — Length

The metre is the base unit of length. Since 2019, it is defined as the distance light travels in a vacuum in exactly 1/299,792,458 of a second. This definition ties the metre to a universal physical constant.

2. Kilogram (kg) — Mass

The kilogram is the base unit of mass. Since 2019, it is defined by fixing the Planck constant (h) to exactly 6.62607015 × 10⁻³⁴ joule-seconds. It replaced the physical prototype kilogram kept in France.

3. Second (s) — Time

The second is defined as exactly 9,192,631,770 oscillations of the caesium-133 atom. This atomic definition gives time measurement unprecedented accuracy.

4. Ampere (A) — Electric Current

The ampere is defined by fixing the elementary charge (e) to exactly 1.602176634 × 10⁻¹⁹ coulombs. One ampere equals the flow of approximately 6.241 × 10¹⁸ electrons per second.

5. Kelvin (K) — Thermodynamic Temperature

The kelvin is defined by fixing the Boltzmann constant (k) to exactly 1.380649 × 10⁻²³ joules per kelvin. 0 K is absolute zero — the theoretical lowest possible temperature.

6. Mole (mol) — Amount of Substance

One mole is exactly 6.02214076 × 10²³ elementary entities (atoms, molecules, etc.) — Avogadro's number. It bridges atomic-scale quantities to laboratory-scale measurements.

7. Candela (cd) — Luminous Intensity

The candela measures the power of light as perceived by the human eye in a given direction. It is defined by fixing the luminous efficacy of monochromatic radiation at 683 lm/W.

SI Prefixes

SI uses a powerful prefix system to express multiples and sub-multiples of units:

  • Yotta (Y) = 10²⁴
  • Giga (G) = 10⁹
  • Mega (M) = 10⁶
  • Kilo (k) = 10³
  • Centi (c) = 10⁻²
  • Milli (m) = 10⁻³
  • Micro (μ) = 10⁻⁶
  • Nano (n) = 10⁻⁹

Why SI Matters

A consistent measurement system is essential for scientific reproducibility, international trade, and engineering safety. Famous measurement failures caused by unit confusion include the loss of the Mars Climate Orbiter in 1999, caused by one team using metric and another using imperial units.

History

History of Measurement Systems

Measurement has been a cornerstone of civilization for more than 5,000 years. From the ancient body-based units of Egypt and Mesopotamia to today's globally standardized SI system, the history of measurement is a story of growing precision, international cooperation, and the occasional catastrophic failure.

Ancient Measurement Systems

The earliest known unit of measurement is the cubit — the length from the elbow to the tip of the middle finger, used in ancient Egypt around 3000 BCE. The Egyptian royal cubit (52.3 cm) was standardized using a physical master stick. The Sumerians used the barleycorn as a unit of length.

Roman units like the mille passuum (a thousand paces, giving us "mile") and Greek units like the stadion (about 200 metres) spread across the ancient world but varied between regions and periods.

Medieval Chaos

The Middle Ages were characterized by extraordinary measurement chaos. In 13th-century England alone, the "bushel" varied in volume by region. In France, over 700 differently-sized "pints" existed. This inconsistency created serious barriers to trade and taxation.

The French Revolution and the Birth of the Metric System

In 1790, the French National Assembly commissioned a new, rational measurement system. Proposed by the French Academy of Sciences, the metric system was officially adopted in France in 1795. The metre was defined as 1/10,000,000 of the distance from the North Pole to the equator through Paris.

The metric system spread through Napoleon's conquests and by 1875, the Metre Convention was signed by 17 nations, creating the International Bureau of Weights and Measures (BIPM).

The Imperial System

Britain's Imperial System, formalized in 1824, unified previously inconsistent English units. When the United States declared independence, it inherited the older English units that predated Imperial standardization — which is why US customary units differ slightly from Imperial ones (e.g., the US gallon is smaller than the Imperial gallon).

The SI System (1960–Present)

The modern International System of Units (SI) was established in 1960 and has been refined continuously since. The landmark 2019 redefinition replaced all physical artifact standards (like the platinum kilogram kept in Paris) with definitions based on fundamental physical constants — making SI truly universal, not tied to any physical object.

Famous Measurement Disasters

  • Mars Climate Orbiter (1999): A $327 million spacecraft was lost because one engineering team used metric units (newton-seconds) while another used imperial units (pound-force-seconds).
  • Gimli Glider (1983): A Boeing 767 ran out of fuel mid-flight because ground crew confused kilograms and pounds when calculating fuel load.
  • Columbus's Navigation Error (1492): Columbus severely underestimated Earth's circumference partly due to confusion between Roman miles and Arabic miles.
Practical Tips

Quick Mental Conversion Tricks

While Omni Converter gives you instant exact results, it's incredibly useful to know rough mental approximations — especially when traveling, cooking, or in situations without a device handy.

Length & Distance

  • km → miles: Multiply km by 0.6 (exact: 0.621371). Rough trick: subtract 1/3. So 30 km ≈ 20 miles.
  • miles → km: Multiply by 1.6. So 5 miles ≈ 8 km.
  • cm → inches: Divide by 2.5. So 10 cm ≈ 4 inches.
  • feet → metres: Multiply by 0.3. So 10 feet ≈ 3 metres.
  • metres → feet: Multiply by 3.3. So 2 metres ≈ 6.6 feet.

Temperature

  • °C → °F (quick): Double the Celsius and add 30. So 20°C ≈ 70°F (exact: 68°F). Close enough for weather!
  • °F → °C (quick): Subtract 30, then halve. So 80°F → (80-30)/2 = 25°C (exact: 26.7°C).
  • Key anchors to memorize: 0°C = 32°F (freezing), 20°C = 68°F (room temp), 37°C = 98.6°F (body temp), 100°C = 212°F (boiling).

Weight & Mass

  • kg → lbs: Multiply by 2.2. So 70 kg ≈ 154 lbs.
  • lbs → kg: Divide by 2.2 (or multiply by 0.45). So 150 lbs ≈ 68 kg.
  • grams → ounces: Divide by 28. So 100g ≈ 3.5 oz.
  • Stone (UK) → kg: Multiply stone by 6.35. So 12 stone = 76 kg.

Volume

  • litres → US gallons: Divide by 3.8. So 20L ≈ 5.2 gallons.
  • US gallons → litres: Multiply by 3.8.
  • ml → fl oz: Divide by 30. So 300ml ≈ 10 fl oz.
  • Cooking — tablespoon to ml: 1 tablespoon ≈ 15 ml.
  • Cooking — cup to ml: 1 US cup = 237 ml (roughly 240 ml).

Speed

  • km/h → mph: Multiply by 0.6 (same as km → miles trick). 100 km/h ≈ 62 mph.
  • mph → km/h: Multiply by 1.6. 60 mph ≈ 96 km/h.
  • m/s → km/h: Multiply by 3.6. So 10 m/s = 36 km/h.

Data Storage

  • 1 KB ≈ 1,000 bytes (or exactly 1,024 bytes in binary)
  • 1 MB ≈ 1 million bytes | 1 GB ≈ 1 billion bytes
  • Quick rule: Each step up the scale (KB→MB→GB→TB) multiplies by ~1,000.

Area

  • m² → ft²: Multiply by 10.76. So 100 m² ≈ 1,076 ft².
  • acres → hectares: 1 acre ≈ 0.4 hectares (or 1 hectare ≈ 2.47 acres).
  • Rough rule: A football field ≈ 0.5 hectares ≈ 1.2 acres ≈ 5,000 m².
